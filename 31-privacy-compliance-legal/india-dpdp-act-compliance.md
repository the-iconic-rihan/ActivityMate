# India DPDP Act 2023 Compliance

## Overview

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act) is India's binding data-protection law and the compliance requirement this entire folder exists to operationalize, per the folder README. This document maps DPDP Act obligations to ActivityMate's actual data footprint and features rather than restating the Act in the abstract. **This document is written by engineering/product for internal planning purposes and does not constitute legal advice; every section marked "Legal Review Needed" requires sign-off from qualified Indian data-protection counsel before ActivityMate relies on it, and the DPDP Act's subordinate Rules were still being finalized as of this document's writing — several mechanics below (breach-notification timelines, Significant Data Fiduciary thresholds, cross-border transfer restrictions) are defined by Rules that may be amended after this document is written.**

## Roles Under the Act

- **Data Fiduciary**: ActivityMate (the company operating the platform) — the entity that determines the purpose and means of processing personal data.
- **Data Principal**: the individual the data is about — every ActivityMate user, whether verified or not.
- **Data Processor**: any third party processing data on ActivityMate's behalf under contract — cloud infrastructure provider, SMS/OTP gateway, identity-verification vendor, payment processor. ActivityMate remains the Data Fiduciary and is accountable for these processors' compliance under the Act, which means processor contracts must include DPDP-compliant data-handling clauses.
- **Consent Manager**: an Act-defined, separately registered entity through which a Data Principal can manage consent across multiple fiduciaries. ActivityMate does not operate as a Consent Manager and does not currently plan to integrate with the Consent Manager ecosystem for MVP — consent is captured directly, per [consent-management-spec.md](consent-management-spec.md). This should be revisited if the Consent Manager ecosystem becomes a practical distribution/trust channel post-PMF.
- **Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)**: the Act's adjudicatory body, which receives breach notifications and grievance escalations and can levy penalties.

## Consent Obligations

The Act requires consent to be **free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous**, given via clear affirmative action, and accompanied by an itemized notice in clear language (English and, practically, the primary local languages ActivityMate supports at launch) stating what data is collected, for what purpose, and how the Data Principal can exercise their rights and withdraw consent. Bundled, pre-ticked, or "by using the app you agree to everything" consent does not meet this bar.

Applied to ActivityMate, this means consent is captured **granularly per data category**, not as a single blanket checkbox:
- Phone/email — required, captured at signup, purpose: account creation and communication.
- Selfie/government ID — required only for the verification tiers the user opts into (per PRD §9, government ID is explicitly optional); consent notice must state the ID is used for verification and, if applicable, retained per [data-retention-deletion-policy.md](data-retention-deletion-policy.md), not repurposed for anything else without fresh consent.
- Precise location — required for check-in verification, with a distinct consent moment from location-based discovery, since these are functionally different purposes even though they both use location.
- Chat data — implicit in using the messaging feature, but the notice must disclose that messages are retained for safety/moderation purposes, not treated as ephemeral.

Consent must be as easy to withdraw as to give. Withdrawal does not retroactively make prior lawful processing unlawful, but it does trigger the erasure obligation below unless another lawful basis (e.g., legal retention for an open safety incident) applies. See [consent-management-spec.md](consent-management-spec.md) for the UI/backend mechanics.

**"Certain Legitimate Uses" exception.** The Act permits limited processing without consent for specified legitimate uses (e.g., a voluntarily-provided-for-a-purpose disclosure, medical emergency, or compliance with law/court order). ActivityMate should not rely on this exception as a substitute for consent for its core features (verification, location, chat) — those are consent-based by design — but the exception is relevant to the law-enforcement cooperation flow in [law-enforcement-request-policy.md](law-enforcement-request-policy.md).

## Data Principal Rights

The Act grants Data Principals the right to:
1. **Access** — a summary of personal data being processed and the processing activities, and identities of any Data Processors it's shared with.
2. **Correction and completion** — update inaccurate or incomplete data (e.g., a user correcting a mistyped name or an outdated location).
3. **Erasure** — request deletion of personal data once the purpose is served and no legal retention basis remains.
4. **Grievance redressal** — a Data Principal can raise a complaint with ActivityMate directly before escalating to the Data Protection Board; the Act requires the Fiduciary to have a **Grievance Officer** and a published, accessible grievance mechanism with response timelines defined by Rules.
5. **Nominate** — a Data Principal can nominate another individual to exercise their rights in the event of death or incapacity — relevant given ActivityMate's safety-incident context.

**Feature-level implementation:** access/correction/erasure requests are handled through an in-app "My Data" flow backed by a defined API contract, not manual support tickets alone, given expected volume at scale. Response timelines are set by Rules under the Act (not fixed by ActivityMate) — *Legal Review Needed: confirm the exact response-window Rule in force at time of implementation and hard-code that SLA into the grievance workflow rather than assuming a number here.*

## Data Fiduciary Obligations

- **Purpose limitation and data minimization.** Data is collected only for the stated purpose and only what is necessary — this directly supports the location-fuzzing design in [../17-maps-location/location-privacy-strategy.md](../17-maps-location/location-privacy-strategy.md) (precise location is minimized to the check-in moment, not retained broadly) and the optional-vs-mandatory tiering of verification data in [PRD-001](../PRD-001_Master_Product_Requirements.md) §9.
- **Accuracy.** Reasonable efforts to keep data accurate and complete, particularly data used to make decisions about a Data Principal (e.g., trust score inputs) — supports the correction right above.
- **Reasonable security safeguards.** Technical and organizational measures to prevent breach — implemented via [../30-security/security-architecture-overview.md](../30-security/security-architecture-overview.md) and [../30-security/secrets-key-management.md](../30-security/secrets-key-management.md). This is the clause most directly enforced by penalty (see below).
- **Erasure on purpose completion or consent withdrawal.** Data must be deleted (or anonymized) when it is no longer necessary for the stated purpose, unless retention is required by law — the exact schedule is in [data-retention-deletion-policy.md](data-retention-deletion-policy.md).
- **Breach notification.** On becoming aware of a personal data breach, the Data Fiduciary must notify the Data Protection Board and affected Data Principals. *Legal Review Needed: the Draft DPDP Rules (as circulated for public comment) proposed notifying the Board without delay with a fuller report within 72 hours, and notifying affected Data Principals without delay in each case — but these timelines were not finalized as of this document's writing. Engineering must build the breach-detection-to-notification pipeline (see [../28-monitoring-observability/alerting-oncall-policy.md](../28-monitoring-observability/alerting-oncall-policy.md) and [../40-incident-disaster-recovery/incident-management-process.md](../40-incident-disaster-recovery/incident-management-process.md)) to be fast enough to meet whatever timeline the final Rules set, treating "hours, not days" as the working assumption.*
- **Grievance Officer.** A named individual/role responsible for responding to Data Principal grievances — should be designated before public launch, published in the privacy policy per [privacy-policy-framework.md](privacy-policy-framework.md).

## Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF) Status

The Act allows the government to notify certain Data Fiduciaries as **Significant Data Fiduciaries** based on factors including volume and sensitivity of data processed, risk to Data Principal rights, and other criteria to be specified. SDF status brings additional obligations: appointing a **Data Protection Officer based in India** who reports to the Board, appointing an **independent data auditor**, and periodically conducting a **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)**.

ActivityMate is very unlikely to be individually notified as an SDF at MVP/single-city scale, but the *nature* of its data (government ID, precise location, biometric-adjacent selfie data at volume) is squarely the kind of processing the SDF category exists to capture, so this should be tracked, not dismissed, as the platform scales city-by-city. *Legal Review Needed: monitor government SDF notifications and re-assess at each city-launch milestone; there is no fixed numeric threshold published as of this document's writing.*

## Cross-Border Data Transfer

The DPDP Act takes a "blacklist" approach rather than GDPR's adequacy-decision model: transfer of personal data outside India is permitted by default **except** to countries the Central Government restricts by notification. As of this document's writing, no such restricted-country list had been notified. This is directly relevant to ActivityMate's tech stack (per [PRD-001](../PRD-001_Master_Product_Requirements.md) §12) if cloud infrastructure, backup storage, third-party SMS/OTP providers, or identity-verification vendors host data or process it outside India.

*Legal Review Needed: (1) confirm final cloud region/vendor choices in [../25-infrastructure-cloud/cloud-provider-evaluation.md](../25-infrastructure-cloud/cloud-provider-evaluation.md) against the DPDP cross-border regime once vendors are selected; (2) monitor for any restricted-country notification; (3) note that other Indian sectoral rules (e.g., RBI data-localization requirements, which would apply if/when [19-payments-subscriptions](../19-payments-subscriptions/README.md) processes payment data) can impose stricter localization than the DPDP Act itself and must be checked independently.*

## Children's Data (Section 9) and the 18+ Decision

Section 9 of the Act imposes strict obligations for processing a child's (under-18) personal data: verifiable parental consent, and a prohibition on tracking, behavioral monitoring, or targeted advertising directed at children. ActivityMate's product decision to operate as an **18-plus-only platform** (see [age-verification-minors-policy.md](age-verification-minors-policy.md)) is partly a safety decision (offline meetups) and partly a decision that sidesteps Section 9's compliance burden entirely — provided age-gating is genuinely enforced, not merely a checkbox. If age verification is weak in practice, ActivityMate could be processing minors' data without the Section 9 safeguards Section 9 requires, which is a compounding legal and safety risk. This makes the enforcement mechanism in [age-verification-minors-policy.md](age-verification-minors-policy.md) load-bearing for DPDP compliance, not just a safety nice-to-have.

## Penalties

The Act's schedule provides for significant financial penalties adjudicated by the Data Protection Board, including up to ₹250 crore for failure to take reasonable security safeguards to prevent a data breach, and up to ₹200 crore for breach of children's-data obligations, among other tiered amounts for other violations. These are cited here to justify why this folder is P0 and why [../30-security](../30-security/README.md) controls are treated as non-negotiable rather than best-effort — the exposure is not hypothetical at ActivityMate's data sensitivity level. *Legal Review Needed: confirm current penalty schedule against the final Act text/Rules, as this document summarizes from the 2023 Act text and penalty amounts should be re-verified before any board/investor-facing risk disclosure.*
