# Culture Values Handbook

**Status:** Draft v1.

## Scope

Expands the values in foundation into day-to-day culture norms.

## Purpose

[mission-vision-values.md](../00-foundation/mission-vision-values.md) states ActivityMate's mission, vision, and operating values at the level a founder or investor reads them. This handbook translates those same values into what they mean **on an ordinary Tuesday** — the actual behaviors, defaults, and tradeoffs employees are expected to apply when nobody is explicitly telling them what to do. A values statement that never gets this concrete stays decorative.

The five [core product principles](../PRD-001_Master_Product_Requirements.md) from the seed PRD — Activity First, Trust Before Matching, Safety Over Growth, Community Driven, Local Network Density — are product principles. This handbook is their people-and-culture mirror: how the *team* should operate so the product principles are actually achievable.

## 1. "Safety Over Growth" as a working norm, not a slogan

**What it means day to day**: When a growth tactic and a safety/trust tactic conflict, safety wins by default, and the person who escalates the conflict is never penalized for slowing something down.

Concretely:
- An engineer who flags "this feature could be used to spam activity invites" gets thanked for raising it in review, not told they're overthinking a growth feature.
- A growth hire who declines to run an acquisition campaign that would flood a launch neighborhood faster than trust & safety can review the resulting verifications is making the right call, not underperforming.
- Nobody ships a change to the trust score or reporting flow without the second-reviewer requirement in [code-review-standards.md](../37-engineering-standards/code-review-standards.md) — this is a cultural expectation as much as a process rule.

**What it doesn't mean**: Safety is not an excuse for indefinite caution or for trust & safety operations to block product velocity without specific, articulable reasons. "Safety over growth" is a tiebreaker for genuine conflicts, not a veto anyone can invoke to avoid making a decision.

## 2. "Activity First, Not Dating-App-First"

We build and talk about the product as infrastructure for real-world activity partnership, not as a matchmaking product with romance filtered out. This shows up in small things: internal language always describes users as "hosts" and "participants," never "matches"; product reviews ask "would this feature work equally well for a same-gender friend group as for a mixed group," a quick test for accidental dating-app framing creeping in.

## 3. Community Driven — the team treats Mumbai users as partners, not a metric

Team members (especially growth, community, and trust & safety roles) are expected to spend real time with actual hosts and participants — attending activities, reading support tickets directly rather than only dashboards, and bringing qualitative texture back into product decisions. A team that only sees users as rows in an analytics table will optimize for the wrong things.

## 4. Local Network Density — depth over breadth, always

We resist the temptation to spread thin across many cities or many activity categories before Mumbai is genuinely dense and trusted. This is a cultural discipline as much as a strategy: saying no to "let's also launch in Bangalore" or "let's also add event ticketing" before the current wedge is proven is expected and rewarded, not seen as lack of ambition. See [go-to-market-strategy.md](../01-business-strategy/go-to-market-strategy.md).

## 5. Directness with care

Trust & safety judgment calls, code review feedback, and performance conversations all require directness — vague or overly softened feedback is a disservice in a company where some judgment calls have real safety stakes. At the same time, directness is delivered with care: feedback addresses the work or the decision, not the person, and is given privately before (if ever) discussed in a group setting.

## 6. Ownership over hierarchy

Given the flat structure through at least Series A (see [hiring-plan-org-chart.md](../36-hiring-people-culture/hiring-plan-org-chart.md)), decisions are made by whoever is closest to the problem and has the relevant context, with escalation to a founder only when a decision is genuinely cross-cutting or high-stakes (safety incidents, legal/compliance questions, spend above a set threshold). Waiting for permission on a decision that's clearly within someone's remit is discouraged.

## 7. Confidentiality as a default, not an event

Because the product handles verification data (selfie, government ID) and safety reports, every employee — not just trust & safety — treats user data with default confidentiality: no screenshotting user profiles or reports for casual internal chat, no discussing specific user incidents outside the relevant reviewing team. This is reinforced in [onboarding-playbook.md](onboarding-playbook.md) from day one.

## 8. How this handbook is used

- Referenced explicitly in the values interview round of [interview-process-rubrics.md](interview-process-rubrics.md).
- Walked through in week one of [onboarding-playbook.md](onboarding-playbook.md).
- Revisited at every all-hands when a real decision tested one of these norms — culture handbooks that are only read once at onboarding decay into wallpaper; this one is meant to be cited in real meetings.

## Open questions

- As the team grows past ~30 people, whether a more formal "values in review" process (e.g. peer feedback explicitly citing these norms) is needed, or whether informal reinforcement remains sufficient — to be revisited by People Ops around the Series A hiring phase.
