# Persona Research Raw Data

## Important Framing Note

Everything below is **illustrative synthesized fieldwork**, constructed to be representative of the persona segments defined in [PRD-001](../PRD-001_Master_Product_Requirements.md) Section 5 and consistent with the behavioral patterns documented in [india-social-behavior-research.md](india-social-behavior-research.md). No named individual, quote, or statistic below corresponds to a real, identifiable person. This document exists to give downstream teams (UX persona synthesis in [../05-ux/](../05-ux/README.md), coded findings in [survey-interview-findings.md](survey-interview-findings.md)) a concrete, realistic body of raw material to work from ahead of a real fielded study, and should be **replaced with actual transcripts and survey exports** as soon as real primary research is conducted. Respondent identifiers below (e.g., "R1") are fieldwork codes, not names.

## Methodology (Illustrative Study Design)

Illustrative design: 18 semi-structured interviews (45-60 min each) across the primary and secondary personas, plus a 240-respondent structured survey distributed through relocation-focused Facebook/WhatsApp groups, co-working spaces, and campus channels in the South Mumbai/BKC/Powai wedge geography defined in [../01-business-strategy/go-to-market-strategy.md](../01-business-strategy/go-to-market-strategy.md). Interview guide referenced: [../03-user-research/interview-guides-templates.md](../03-user-research/interview-guides-templates.md) (onboarding-motivation guide). Recruitment skewed toward the 25-34 age band per the go-to-market wedge persona, with secondary sampling across students and solo travellers.

## Interview Excerpts — Relocated Professional Persona

**R1 — 27, product marketing, relocated from Pune to Powai for a new job, 4 months in Mumbai.**

> "My office is fine, people are nice, but everyone already has their own weekend plans, their own college friends who are also in Mumbai. I'm not going to invite myself. I've tried a couple of the running club WhatsApp groups near my building — one was basically dead, admin hadn't posted in weeks. Another one was active but felt like a closed clique already, nobody really greeted the new person. I ended up going for a solo run instead, which is fine, but Saturday evenings are the hard part. I don't want to just sit in my flat but I also don't want to go to a bar alone and awkwardly stand around."

**R4 — 31, finance, relocated from Delhi to BKC, 8 months in Mumbai, lives alone.**

> "I actually joined three WhatsApp groups from a 'new to Mumbai' Facebook page. One is just noise now — recruitment ads and random forwards. The other two, I've met maybe two people in person total, and honestly it felt a bit like a blind date logistically — where do we even meet, is this person going to be normal, do I tell my flatmate where I'm going just in case. If there was something that showed me — this person has actually done this ten times before with other people and got good ratings — I'd trust it a lot more than a random WhatsApp admin vouching for them."

**R7 — 29, remote software engineer relocated from Bengaluru to South Mumbai, 6 months in.**

> "I work from home for a Bengaluru-based company so I have zero office colleagues in this city at all. My social life is basically Discord servers for work stuff and my old college group chat, none of whom are in Mumbai. I've thought about co-working spaces just for the social angle, not because I need the desk. What I actually want is something low-effort — like, I don't want to plan an entire evening, I want to say 'I'm free tonight, anyone want to grab dinner near Bandra' and have that be a normal, safe thing to post."

## Interview Excerpts — Student Persona

**S2 — 22, master's student, business school near Powai, from Kochi originally, first year in Mumbai.**

> "Campus is easy for the first few weeks because everyone's new together, but after the first semester, friend groups solidify and if you weren't in the right hostel wing or the right project group, you're kind of on the outside. Also a lot of my classmates are from Mumbai already and go home most weekends, so weekends specifically get quiet. I'd use something like this mostly for exploring the city — like a photography walk or trying a new food area — stuff that's more fun with company but doesn't require deep friendship first."

**S5 — 24, hostel resident, engineering college near Powai, from Nagpur.**

> "Honestly for me it's less about loneliness and more about safety logistics. My parents are very particular that I shouldn't go out at night alone, especially somewhere new. If there was an app where I could show them 'look, this is a verified group activity, other verified people are going, here's the venue' that would actually make it easier for me to convince them to let me go out more, not just easier for me personally."

## Interview Excerpts — Solo Traveller / Freelancer Persona

**F3 — 34, freelance photographer, moved to Mumbai for project work, staying 6-8 months, no fixed office.**

> "I move around a lot for work and Mumbai is new to me. I don't have the patience to slowly build a social circle over a year because I might not even be here that long. What I want is something more immediate — this weekend, I want to go do X, who else wants to. I've used Meetup before in other cities and honestly the events are hit or miss, sometimes it's just three awkward people standing around a dead group chat that never really planned anything properly."

**F6 — 30, independent consultant, frequent short-term Mumbai visitor, based part-time in Mumbai and part-time in Pune.**

> "I don't need a huge network, I need three or four reliable people for badminton or dinner when I'm in town. Ratings matter a lot to me actually — if I see someone has done 15 activities and has good ratings and no red flags, I'll trust that more than someone with zero history, even if their profile looks nice. Also I'd want to know upfront this isn't a dating thing, because I've had that confusion with a Bumble BFF match before — the guy clearly thought it was a date."

## Interview Excerpts — Secondary Personas (Community Organizers)

**C1 — 36, runs an existing informal Mumbai trekking WhatsApp group (~400 members).**

> "My biggest headache is no-shows. People RSVP in the group, I book a cab or coordinate transport assuming 12 people, and 4 show up. I've started asking for a small refundable deposit just to filter out non-serious people, which some people resent. If a platform could show me someone's actual track record — how often they've followed through before — I'd trust that a lot more than a green tick emoji reply in a group chat."

**C4 — 28, organizes a monthly board games meetup, runs it mostly through Instagram DMs and a shared spreadsheet.**

> "It's genuinely a lot of manual admin work — screening randoms who DM me, making sure it doesn't turn creepy for the women who come, managing a waitlist by hand. If there was a tool that handled verification and let people rate their experience afterward, that would save me hours a month and probably make new attendees feel safer coming to something run by a stranger like me."

## Synthesized Survey Findings (Illustrative, n≈240, Mumbai Wedge Geography)

| Question | Result |
|---|---|
| "In the last 3 months, have you skipped an activity you wanted to do because you had no one to go with?" | ~68% yes |
| "How do you currently find people for casual activities?" (multi-select) | WhatsApp groups 74%, office colleagues 61%, Instagram/social 38%, dating apps repurposed 22%, Meetup-style apps 9% |
| "Would you feel comfortable meeting someone from an app for a daytime group activity (3+ people)?" | 71% yes |
| "Would you feel comfortable meeting someone from an app for a 1:1 evening activity?" | 24% yes (female respondents: 14%; male respondents: 34%) |
| "What would make you trust a stranger enough to meet them in person?" (top themes) | Verified ID/selfie (58%), mutual connection shown (49%), visible activity history/ratings (44%), public venue default (41%) |
| "Have you ever been ghosted/no-showed by someone you arranged to meet via a WhatsApp/Facebook group?" | 52% yes |
| "Would you pay a small subscription for a verified, safety-focused version of this?" | 33% yes outright, 41% "maybe, depends on price," 26% no |
| Average reported number of "new" friends made in Mumbai in first year after relocating | 3.2 |

## Cross-Cutting Observations From This Raw Set

- The gendered safety asymmetry appears sharply in the 1:1-evening-comfort figures (14% female vs 34% male) and is the single most actionable finding for trust and safety design — see [../03-user-research/trust-perception-research.md](../03-user-research/trust-perception-research.md).
- No-show/ghosting frustration (52% survey, plus C1 and R-series interviews) validates cancellation-rate and response-rate as trust score components per PRD-001 Section 9.
- Organizers (C1, C4) independently arrived at ad-hoc verification/accountability workarounds (deposits, manual screening) — validating that there is unmet demand for the product's core trust mechanism from the supply side, not just the demand side.
- Explicit "not a dating app" confusion (F6) recurs and reinforces the naming and positioning caution in [../00-foundation/naming-brand-story.md](../00-foundation/naming-brand-story.md).
- Willingness to pay is real but soft (33% yes / 41% maybe), supporting the freemium-first staged monetization decision in [../01-business-strategy/revenue-model-monetization-strategy.md](../01-business-strategy/revenue-model-monetization-strategy.md) rather than a paywall-first approach.
